BIAS

a. Indicator Introduction

BIAS is derived from Granville Rules. Granville Rules do not exactly express the point where the price starts to move towards the moving average, for it is relative with the strength of market, and BIAS is developed to measure the distance. So BIAS can express the average distance between intraday Index or stock price and the moving average.

BIAS is divided into positive and negative BIAS. If the stock price is above the average line, it is positive; while the stock price is below the average line, it is negative; and when the price crosses average line, it is zero. The larger the positive BIAS is, the more the short-term profit is and the bigger the possibility of the profit- taking is; while the larger the negative BIAS is, the bigger the possibility of the short covering is.

b. Usage Principle

a) When positive 10-day BIAS is larger than 8%, it is a signal of overbuying and it's time to sell; when it is below 8%, it is a phenomenon of overselling and it's time to buy.

b) When positive 30-day BIAS is larger than 16%, it is a phenomenon of overbuying and the time to sell; when it is below 16%, it is a signal of overselling and it's time to buy.

c) When the long and the short powers struggle with each other, BIAS and other average lines are easy to run to the high side, but it is infrequent.

d) When BIAS of price and 10-day average line reaches the max percentage, it will move to zero, maybe below or above zero, it is normal.

e) When BIAS is negative in the main ascending trend, we can open a position after price falls back; while BIAS is positive in the main descending trend, we can close it after price rises high.
